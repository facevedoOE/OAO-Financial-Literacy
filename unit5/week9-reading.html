SECTION 1
What Is Credit?

Credit is an agreement: someone lends you money or lets you use something now, and you promise to pay them back later — usually with interest. Every time you swipe a credit card, take out a car loan, or even set up a payment plan for a phone, you're using credit.

📘 VOCABULARY

Credit — The ability to borrow money or access goods/services with the agreement to pay later, typically with interest.

Creditor — The person, bank, or company that lends money or extends credit. Also called a lender.

Credit isn't inherently good or bad — it's a tool. Used wisely, credit helps you build a financial reputation, make large purchases (like a car or home), and handle emergencies. Used poorly, it can lead to crushing debt, damaged credit scores, and years of financial stress.

The difference between people who benefit from credit and those who are hurt by it usually comes down to one thing: understanding how it works before you use it.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

The average American household carries over $6,000 in credit card debt. At a typical 22% APR, making only minimum payments on that balance would take over 17 years to pay off — and cost more than $8,000 in interest alone. Understanding credit before you start using it is one of the best financial decisions you can make.

SECTION 2
Credit Cards: Benefits and Pitfalls

A credit card lets you borrow money from a card issuer (like Visa, Mastercard, or a bank) up to a set limit. Each month, you receive a statement showing what you owe. If you pay the full balance by the due date, you pay zero interest. If you pay less than the full balance, the remaining amount carries over and starts accruing interest.

📘 VOCABULARY

Credit Limit — The maximum amount a credit card company allows you to borrow at any time.

Statement Balance — The total amount you owe at the end of a billing cycle.

Minimum Payment — The smallest amount you must pay by the due date to avoid late fees (usually 1–3% of the balance or $25, whichever is greater).

✅ Benefits of Credit Cards
Build credit history — Responsible use builds your credit score, which you'll need for apartments, car loans, and more.
Fraud protection — Federal law limits your liability to $50 for unauthorized charges (most cards offer $0 liability). Debit cards don't offer the same level of protection.
Rewards — Many cards offer cash back, points, or travel rewards on purchases you'd make anyway.
Emergency backup — Provides a safety net when unexpected expenses arise and cash isn't available.
🚩 Common Pitfalls
Carrying a balance — Not paying in full each month means you're charged interest, often at 18–27% APR. This is how debt spirals.
Minimum payment trap — Paying only the minimum keeps you in debt for years and costs a fortune in interest.
Overspending — Credit makes spending feel painless because you don't see money leave your account immediately.
Fees — Late payment fees ($25–$40), annual fees, cash advance fees, and over-limit fees can add up quickly.

SECTION 3
Interest Rates and APR

When you borrow money, the lender charges you a fee for using their money. That fee is the interest rate. But not all rates are expressed the same way, so you need to understand the difference between an interest rate and an APR.

📘 VOCABULARY

Interest Rate — The percentage charged on borrowed money over a specific period, not including fees.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) — The total yearly cost of borrowing, including the interest rate plus any additional fees. APR gives you a more complete picture of what borrowing actually costs.

Grace Period — The time between the end of a billing cycle and the payment due date during which no interest is charged if you pay in full. Typically 21–25 days.

For credit cards, the APR is especially important because it determines how much interest you're charged on any balance you carry from month to month. Here's how to estimate monthly interest cost:

Monthly Interest Calculation
Step 1: Divide APR by 12 to get the monthly rate
Step 2: Multiply the monthly rate by your balance

Example: $1,500 balance at 24% APR
Monthly rate: 24% ÷ 12 = 2% per month
Monthly interest: $1,500 × 0.02 = $30/month in interest

That $30 doesn't reduce your balance — it's added to it. If you only pay the minimum, most of your payment goes toward interest, and the actual balance barely shrinks. This is exactly how credit card debt traps people for years.

SECTION 4
Credit Reports and FICO Scores

Your credit report is like a financial report card — it tracks how you've handled borrowed money over time. Your credit score is a single number that summarizes that report. Together, they determine whether lenders will trust you with credit and what interest rate you'll get.

📘 VOCABULARY

Credit Report — A detailed record of your credit history maintained by three major bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. It includes accounts, payment history, balances, and public records.

FICO Score — A credit score ranging from 300 to 850 that lenders use to assess your creditworthiness. Higher scores qualify for lower interest rates and better terms.

Credit Utilization — The percentage of your available credit that you're currently using. Calculated: (balance ÷ credit limit) × 100. Lower is better — experts recommend staying below 30%.

How Your FICO Score Is Calculated
Payment History 35%
Do you pay on time? This is the single most important factor. Even one late payment can significantly lower your score.
Amounts Owed (Utilization) 30%
How much of your available credit are you using? Using less than 30% of your credit limit shows you're not over-relying on borrowed money.
Length of Credit History 15%
How long have your accounts been open? Longer histories are better, which is why starting early and keeping accounts open matters.
Credit Mix 10%
Do you have different types of credit (cards, loans, etc.)? A diverse mix shows you can handle various credit responsibly.
New Credit Inquiries 10%
How many new accounts have you applied for recently? Many applications in a short time can signal financial distress.

Building Credit as a Young Adult
✓ Become an authorized user on a parent's credit card
✓ Open a secured credit card (requires a deposit as collateral)
✓ Pay every bill on time — every single time
✓ Keep credit utilization below 30%
✓ Check your credit report annually at AnnualCreditReport.com (free)
✓ Don't apply for multiple cards at once
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

You're entitled to one free credit report per year from each of the three major bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) at AnnualCreditReport.com. This is the only federally authorized source — other sites that claim to offer "free" reports often try to sell you a subscription.
