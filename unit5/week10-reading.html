SECTION 1
Types of Loans

At some point, almost everyone borrows money. Whether it's for college, a car, or an emergency, understanding the different types of loans helps you choose wisely — and avoid the ones designed to trap you.

📘 VOCABULARY

Secured Loan — A loan backed by collateral (an asset the lender can take if you don't pay). Example: auto loan — the car is collateral.

Unsecured Loan — A loan with no collateral. The lender relies on your creditworthiness. Example: personal loan, credit card.

Collateral — An asset (car, house, savings) pledged as security for a loan. If you default, the lender can seize it.

Auto Loans
Type:Secured (car is collateral) Typical APR:4%–7% (good credit) to 14%+ (poor credit) Terms:36, 48, 60, or 72 months Key tip:Shorter terms = higher payments but less total interest
Student Loans
Type:Unsecured Federal APR:~5%–7% (fixed, set by Congress) Private APR:3%–14%+ (varies by lender and credit) Key tip:Always exhaust federal loans first — they offer income-driven repayment and forgiveness options that private loans don't
📘 VOCABULARY

Subsidized Loan — A federal student loan where the government pays the interest while you're in school. Available based on financial need.

Unsubsidized Loan — A federal student loan where interest accrues from the moment you receive the money, even while you're still in school.

Personal Loans
Type:Usually unsecured Typical APR:6%–36% depending on credit score Use cases:Debt consolidation, large purchases, emergencies Key tip:Shop around — rates vary dramatically between lenders
🚩 Payday Loans — The Debt Trap
Type:Unsecured, very short-term Typical APR:300%–500%+ (not a typo) Terms:2 weeks (due on your next payday) How it works:Borrow $300, owe $345–$375 in two weeks. Can't pay? Roll it over — now you owe fees on top of fees.

Payday loans target people who are already financially stressed and make the situation dramatically worse. They're legal in Florida but heavily criticized by consumer advocates. Nearly 80% of payday loans are rolled over or followed by another loan within 14 days, creating a cycle of debt that's extremely hard to escape.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

A typical payday loan charges $15 per $100 borrowed for a 2-week term. That's a 391% APR. If you borrowed $500, you'd owe $575 in just two weeks. Compare that to a credit card at 22% APR, where $500 costs about $9 in monthly interest.

SECTION 2
Amortization: How Loan Payments Work

When you make a loan payment each month, the money doesn't all go to paying off what you borrowed. It's split between principal (the amount you borrowed) and interest (the lender's fee). The way this split changes over time is called amortization.

📘 VOCABULARY

Amortization — The process of spreading loan payments over time so that each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal, gradually reducing the balance to zero.

Amortization Schedule — A table showing each payment's breakdown between principal and interest over the life of the loan.

Here's the key insight: early payments are mostly interest. As you pay down the balance, more of each payment goes toward principal. This is why making extra payments early in a loan saves the most money.

SECTION 3
Predatory Lending and How to Avoid It

Predatory lending describes unfair, deceptive, or abusive loan practices that benefit the lender at the borrower's expense. Predatory lenders target people who are desperate, financially uneducated, or have poor credit — offering "easy" money with devastating terms hidden in the fine print.

📘 VOCABULARY

Predatory Lending — Unfair or deceptive loan practices that impose unreasonable terms on borrowers, often targeting those with limited financial literacy or few alternatives.

Red Flags of Predatory Lending
🚩 Extremely high APR — Rates above 36% are a red flag. Payday and title loans often exceed 300% APR.
🚩 Pressure to sign quickly — "This offer expires today!" Legitimate lenders give you time to read terms and compare.
🚩 Hidden fees and penalties — Origination fees, prepayment penalties, and balloon payments buried in fine print.
🚩 "No credit check" advertising — This usually means extremely high rates. They don't check credit because they profit from your inability to repay.
🚩 Loan flipping / refinancing pressure — Encouraging you to refinance repeatedly, adding new fees each time while the balance barely decreases.
📘 VOCABULARY

Prepayment Penalty — A fee charged by some lenders if you pay off a loan early. This discourages borrowers from escaping high-interest debt.

Balloon Payment — A large lump-sum payment due at the end of a loan term, often much larger than the regular monthly payments.

How to Protect Yourself
✓ Always compare at least 3 loan offers before accepting one
✓ Read the full loan agreement — especially the fine print about fees and penalties
✓ Check the APR, not just the monthly payment (low payments can mean longer terms and more total interest)
✓ Avoid any lender that pressures you to sign immediately
✓ Never borrow from a lender that advertises "no credit check required"
✓ Build an emergency fund so you're less likely to need desperate borrowing

REFLECTION
Borrow Smart, Stay Free

Debt isn't always bad — but bad debt is always costly. Before borrowing, ask: Do I need this? Can I afford the payments? Have I compared offers? Am I reading the fine print? These four questions can save you thousands.
