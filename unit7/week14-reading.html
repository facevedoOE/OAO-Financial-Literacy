SECTION 1
Identity Theft and Fraud Prevention

Identity theft happens when someone steals your personal information — Social Security number, credit card numbers, bank account details — and uses it to commit fraud. It's one of the fastest-growing crimes in the U.S., and young adults are increasingly targeted because they're active online and often don't monitor their credit.

📘 VOCABULARY

Identity Theft — The fraudulent use of someone's personal information (SSN, credit card numbers, bank accounts) without their consent, typically for financial gain.

Credit Freeze — A security measure that restricts access to your credit report, preventing anyone (including you) from opening new credit accounts until the freeze is lifted. Free to place and remove.

Common Types of Identity Theft
🚩 Financial Identity Theft — Someone opens credit cards, takes out loans, or makes purchases using your identity. You get the bills and the damaged credit.
🚩 Social Media Identity Theft — Someone creates fake accounts using your name and photos to scam your contacts, catfish others, or damage your reputation.
🚩 Tax Identity Theft — Someone files a tax return using your SSN to steal your tax refund. You find out when your real return is rejected.
🚩 Medical Identity Theft — Someone uses your insurance information to get medical care. This can corrupt your medical records and affect future treatment.
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

People ages 18–29 report identity theft at higher rates than any other age group. Why? They share more personal data online, use more apps and services, and are less likely to regularly check their credit reports or bank statements.

How to Protect Yourself
✓ Use strong, unique passwords for every account (use a password manager)
✓ Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all financial accounts
✓ Never share your SSN unless absolutely necessary and you've verified who's asking
✓ Monitor your bank accounts weekly for unauthorized transactions
✓ Check your credit report annually at AnnualCreditReport.com
✓ Place a credit freeze with all three bureaus (free and reversible)
✓ Be cautious on public Wi-Fi — avoid accessing bank accounts or making purchases
✓ Shred documents containing personal financial information
If You're a Victim: Steps to Take
1
Place a fraud alert or credit freeze with all three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion).
2
Report the theft to the FTC at IdentityTheft.gov and file a recovery plan.
3
Contact your bank and credit card companies to report unauthorized transactions.
4
File a police report (some creditors require this for fraud disputes).
5
Change all passwords and enable 2FA on every account.

SECTION 2
Emergency Funds as Risk Management

You learned about emergency funds in Unit 4. Now let's look at them through the lens of risk management. An emergency fund is essentially self-insurance — it protects you from financial shocks that insurance doesn't cover.

Insurance covers specific, defined risks (car accidents, hospitalizations). But many financial emergencies fall outside insurance coverage: losing your job, unexpected car repairs below your deductible, a broken phone, or a family emergency requiring travel. Your emergency fund catches everything else.

📘 VOCABULARY

Risk Management — The process of identifying, assessing, and reducing financial threats through strategies like insurance, emergency savings, diversification, and fraud prevention.

The Three-Layer Protection System
Layer 1: Emergency Fund — Covers unexpected expenses and income disruptions. 3–6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield savings account.
Layer 2: Insurance — Covers catastrophic losses you can't absorb yourself. Health, auto, renters, and eventually life insurance.
Layer 3: Prevention — Reduces the chance of losses in the first place. Safe driving, identity protection, home safety, healthy habits.
🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

When Insurance Isn't Enough

Andre, 21, in Fort Lauderdale has auto insurance with a $1,000 deductible. His car needs $800 in repairs after hitting a pothole — below his deductible, so insurance won't cover it.

WITH Emergency Fund
Pays $800 from savings. Car fixed in days. No stress, no debt. Rebuilds the fund over next few months.
WITHOUT Emergency Fund
Puts $800 on a credit card at 22% APR. Takes months to pay off. Pays $100+ in interest. Stress and debt spiral.

SECTION 3
Evaluating Insurance Policies

Not all insurance policies are created equal. Learning to read and compare policies is a critical skill that can save you thousands of dollars and prevent nasty surprises when you need coverage most.

📘 VOCABULARY

Coverage Limit — The maximum amount an insurance company will pay for a covered loss. Anything above the limit is your responsibility.

Exclusion — A specific situation, event, or condition that is NOT covered by your insurance policy. Always read exclusions carefully before purchasing.

Rider — An add-on to a standard insurance policy that provides extra coverage for specific items or situations (e.g., a jewelry rider on renters insurance).

Questions to Ask When Evaluating a Policy
→ What exactly is covered? (Read the declarations page)
→ What is excluded? (Floods, earthquakes, and acts of war are common exclusions)
→ What is the deductible? Can I afford it if I need to file a claim?
→ What are the coverage limits? Are they high enough for my situation?
→ What is the total annual cost (premiums × 12)?
→ What is the claims process? How do I file, and how quickly do they pay?
→ What discounts are available? (Bundling, good student, safe driver, autopay)
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

Most standard homeowners and renters insurance policies in Florida do NOT cover flood damage. Since Florida is one of the most flood-prone states, residents often need a separate flood insurance policy through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or a private insurer. Always check your exclusions.

🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

Reading the Fine Print

Sofia found a renters insurance policy for $12/month — the cheapest she could find. But she didn't read the exclusions. When her laptop was stolen from her car, she discovered the policy excluded theft from vehicles. A $15/month policy from another company would have covered it. The $3/month savings cost her a $1,200 laptop. The lesson: the cheapest policy isn't always the best value — read the exclusions.
