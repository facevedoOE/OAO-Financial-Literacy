SECTION 1
How Insurance Works

No matter how carefully you plan, unexpected events happen: car accidents, medical emergencies, storms that damage property, or theft. Insurance is a financial tool that protects you from catastrophic losses you couldn't afford on your own.

Here's the basic idea: you pay a regular fee (called a premium) to an insurance company. In return, the company agrees to cover certain financial losses if something bad happens. You're essentially transferring the risk from yourself to the insurance company.

📘 VOCABULARY

Insurance — A contract (policy) in which you pay regular premiums to a company that agrees to cover specific financial losses.

Premium — The regular payment (monthly, quarterly, or annually) you make to keep your insurance policy active.

Deductible — The amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance starts covering costs. Higher deductible = lower premium, but more risk to you.

Claim — A formal request you make to your insurance company asking them to pay for a covered loss.

Copay — A fixed amount you pay for a specific service (like a $25 doctor visit) while insurance covers the rest. Common in health insurance.

The Premium–Deductible Trade-Off

One of the most important decisions in choosing insurance is the balance between your premium and your deductible. They work like a seesaw:

HIGH Premium / LOW Deductible
Pay more each month, but pay less out of pocket when you file a claim. Better if you expect frequent claims.
LOW Premium / HIGH Deductible
Pay less each month, but pay more out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Better if you rarely file claims.
🌎 REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

How a Deductible Works

Jaylen has auto insurance with a $500 deductible. He's in an accident that causes $3,000 in damage to his car.

Total damage:
$3,000
Jaylen pays (deductible):
$500
Insurance pays:
$2,500

Without insurance, Jaylen would owe $3,000. With it, he only pays $500. That's the value of insurance — turning a potentially devastating financial hit into a manageable expense.

SECTION 2
Types of Insurance
Health Insurance

Health insurance covers medical expenses: doctor visits, hospital stays, prescriptions, surgery, preventive care, and mental health services. In the U.S., medical costs without insurance can be devastating — a single emergency room visit can cost $2,000–$5,000+, and a hospital stay can exceed $10,000 per day.

How to get it:Through an employer, parent's plan (until age 26), or the Health Insurance Marketplace (healthcare.gov) Key terms:Premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, out-of-pocket maximum Florida note:Florida did not expand Medicaid, so the Marketplace is the main option for uninsured adults
📘 VOCABULARY

Coinsurance — After meeting your deductible, you and the insurer split remaining costs by percentage (e.g., you pay 20%, insurer pays 80%).

Out-of-Pocket Maximum — The most you'll pay in a year for covered services. After reaching this limit, insurance covers 100% of remaining costs.

Auto Insurance

Auto insurance is required by law in Florida and every other state. It protects you financially if you're in an accident, whether you cause it or not.

Required in FL:$10,000 Personal Injury Protection (PIP) + $10,000 Property Damage Liability Liability:Pays for damage/injuries you cause to others Collision:Pays for damage to your own car in an accident Comprehensive:Covers non-collision damage: theft, vandalism, hurricanes, flooding, falling objects
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

Florida is one of the most expensive states for auto insurance, largely due to its high rate of uninsured drivers, frequent hurricanes, and heavy traffic. The average annual cost for full coverage in Florida is over $3,000 — but rates vary hugely based on your driving record, age, vehicle, and credit score.

Renters Insurance

If you rent an apartment or house, your landlord's insurance covers the building — but not your stuff inside it. Renters insurance protects your personal belongings (electronics, clothes, furniture) from theft, fire, water damage, and other covered events. It also provides liability coverage if someone is injured in your apartment.

Avg. cost:$15–$30/month (one of the cheapest insurance types) Covers:Personal property, liability, additional living expenses if displacement occurs Key tip:Take photos or video of everything you own and store it in the cloud — this makes claims much easier
Life Insurance

Life insurance pays a sum of money (called a death benefit) to your beneficiaries (usually family members) if you die. It's designed to replace your income and help your loved ones cover expenses. Most teens don't need life insurance yet, but understanding it now prepares you for when you have dependents.

📘 VOCABULARY

Term Life Insurance — Coverage for a specific period (10, 20, or 30 years). If you die during the term, your beneficiaries receive the death benefit. Affordable and straightforward.

Whole Life Insurance — Covers you for your entire life and includes a savings component (cash value). More expensive than term life.

TERM LIFE

🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

Maya's First Apartment

Maya, 19, just moved into her first apartment in Tampa. Her parents help her figure out what insurance she needs:

✅ Health insurance: Still on her parents' plan until age 26.
✅ Auto insurance: Her own policy now — required by Florida law. ~$200/month for full coverage.
✅ Renters insurance: $18/month to protect her electronics, furniture, and clothing. Her landlord's insurance doesn't cover her belongings.
⏸ Life insurance: Not needed yet — no dependents relying on her income.
