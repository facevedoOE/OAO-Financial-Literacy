SECTION 1
The Time Value of Money

Here's a question that sounds simple but carries a huge financial lesson: Would you rather have $100 today or $100 one year from now?

Most people instinctively choose the money now — and they're right. But the reason goes deeper than just wanting cash in your pocket. The time value of money is one of the most important concepts in all of personal finance, and it explains why saving early matters so much.

📘 VOCABULARY

Time Value of Money (TVM) — The principle that money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future because it has the potential to earn interest or returns over time.

Why is $100 today worth more than $100 a year from now? Three reasons:

Earning Potential — If you save $100 today at 5% interest, you'll have $105 in one year. The future $100 is just $100.
Inflation — Prices rise over time. What $100 buys today, it may take $103–$105 to buy next year. Future money has less purchasing power.
Opportunity — Money today can be invested, used to avoid debt, or put toward goals. Money you don't have yet can't do any of those things.
📘 VOCABULARY

Inflation — The gradual increase in prices over time, which reduces the purchasing power of money. If inflation is 3%, something that costs $100 today will cost $103 next year.

This is why starting to save now — even small amounts — is so powerful. The earlier your money starts earning interest, the more time it has to grow. A 16-year-old who saves $50/month has a massive head start over someone who waits until they're 30.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

If you saved just $50/month starting at age 16 and earned an average return of 7% per year, you'd have over $190,000 by age 55. Wait until age 25 to start, and you'd have about $91,000 — less than half — even though you only waited 9 years. Time is your most powerful financial tool.

SECTION 2
Simple vs. Compound Interest

When you put money in a savings account, the bank pays you for the privilege of holding your money. That payment is called interest. But not all interest works the same way, and the difference between the two types can mean thousands of dollars over your lifetime.

Simple Interest
📘 VOCABULARY

Simple Interest — Interest calculated only on the original principal (the amount you deposited). Formula: I = P × r × t

Principal — The original amount of money saved or invested, before any interest is earned.

With simple interest, you earn interest only on your original deposit — the principal. The interest earned each year stays the same.

🌎 REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Simple Interest Calculation

Naomi deposits $1,000 in an account earning 5% simple interest per year.

Formula: I = P × r × t
Year 1: $1,000 × 0.05 × 1 = $50 → Balance: $1,050
Year 2: $1,000 × 0.05 × 1 = $50 → Balance: $1,100
Year 3: $1,000 × 0.05 × 1 = $50 → Balance: $1,150
Year 5: $1,000 × 0.05 × 5 = $250 → Balance: $1,250

Notice: Naomi earns exactly $50 every year, regardless of what's in the account. The interest is always calculated on the original $1,000.

Compound Interest
📘 VOCABULARY

Compound Interest — Interest calculated on both the original principal AND the accumulated interest from previous periods. Formula: A = P(1 + r/n)nt

Compound interest is where the magic happens. Instead of earning interest only on your original deposit, you earn interest on your interest. Each period, your balance grows, and then the next round of interest is calculated on that larger balance.

🌎 REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

Compound Interest Calculation

Naomi deposits the same $1,000 but this time at 5% compound interest (compounded annually).

Year 1: $1,000 × 1.05 = $1,050.00
Year 2: $1,050 × 1.05 = $1,102.50
Year 3: $1,102.50 × 1.05 = $1,157.63
Year 5: $1,000 × (1.05)5 = $1,276.28
Year 10: $1,000 × (1.05)10 = $1,628.89

With compound interest, Naomi earns $276.28 after 5 years (vs. $250 with simple). After 10 years, she has $628.89 in interest — more than $128 extra compared to simple interest. And the gap only grows wider over time.

📘 VOCABULARY

Annual Percentage Yield (APY) — The total amount of interest you earn on a deposit over one year, including the effect of compounding. A higher APY means your money grows faster.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Whether he actually said it or not, the point stands — compound interest is one of the most powerful forces in building wealth, and it rewards those who start early.

SECTION 3
The Opportunity Cost of Spending vs. Saving

In Unit 3, you learned about opportunity cost — the value of the next best alternative you give up. Now let's apply that concept to the biggest financial decision you'll face every day: Should I spend this money or save it?

Every dollar you spend today is a dollar that can't earn interest, grow through compounding, or be there when you need it. That doesn't mean you should never spend money — but it means you should understand what you're giving up.

🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

The $5 Coffee Question

Tyler, a senior in Tampa, buys a $5 specialty coffee every school day. That's about $100/month or $1,200/year.

SPEND IT
Enjoys daily coffee
$1,200/year gone
$0 saved
No compound growth
SAVE IT
Makes coffee at home
$1,200/year saved
At 7% for 10 years: ~$17,500
For 30 years: ~$122,000

The point isn't that Tyler should never buy coffee. It's that understanding what that $5/day could become over time helps him make an informed choice. Maybe he buys coffee twice a week instead of five times and saves the difference.

🧠 THINK ABOUT IT

Think about something you spend money on regularly — fast food, streaming services, in-app purchases. What's the monthly cost? If you saved that amount instead and invested it at 7% for 10 years, how much would you have? (Use the formula or estimate.)

Smart financial decisions aren't about eliminating all spending — they're about balancing present enjoyment with future security. The key is to be intentional: know what you're giving up, and decide if it's worth it.

SECTION 4
Building Your Savings: Goals and Emergency Funds

Understanding why saving matters is step one. Step two is having a plan. There are two main types of savings every person should prioritize.

Emergency Fund
📘 VOCABULARY

Emergency Fund — Money set aside in a liquid, accessible account specifically for unexpected expenses like car repairs, medical bills, or job loss. Most experts recommend 3–6 months of essential expenses.

Life is unpredictable. Your car breaks down. You need to replace your phone. You lose hours at work. An emergency fund is a financial safety net that keeps these surprises from becoming financial crises.

For high school students, a good starting goal is $500–$1,000 in an emergency fund. That's enough to cover most unexpected expenses without going into debt or borrowing from parents.

Goal-Based Saving

Beyond emergencies, savings work best when they're tied to specific goals. Giving your money a purpose makes you more likely to stick with your plan.

Short-term goals (under 1 year) — Concert tickets, new shoes, holiday gifts, prom expenses. Save in a regular savings account for easy access.
Medium-term goals (1–5 years) — A car, first apartment deposit, study-abroad trip. Consider a high-yield savings account to earn more interest.
Long-term goals (5+ years) — College costs, starting a business, retirement. Look at CDs, investment accounts, or other growth options (more in Unit 6).
🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

Sofia's Savings Plan

Sofia, a 10th grader in Pensacola, earns $480/month. After budgeting for expenses, she has $120/month to save. Here's how she splits it:

Emergency fund
$40/month
Car savings (medium-term)
$50/month
Concert fund (short-term)
$30/month

By splitting her savings across goals, Sofia makes progress on multiple priorities at once. In a year, she'll have $480 in her emergency fund, $600 toward a car, and $360 for entertainment — all while being intentional about her money.

📘 VOCABULARY

Pay Yourself First — A savings strategy where you set aside money for savings before paying bills or spending on anything else, treating savings as a non-negotiable "expense."

One of the best saving strategies is called "pay yourself first." Instead of saving whatever's left at the end of the month (usually nothing), you move money into savings immediately when you get paid. If your paycheck is $480, you transfer $120 to savings before you spend a dime. What's left is what you live on.
