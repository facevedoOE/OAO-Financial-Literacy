SECTION 1
Thinking Before You Buy: Cost-Benefit Analysis

Every purchase you make is a trade-off. When you spend $60 on new shoes, that's $60 you can't spend on something else — or save for the future. Smart consumers don't just ask "Can I afford this?" They ask "Is this worth it?"

That's the core of cost-benefit analysis — a simple decision-making tool that weighs what you give up against what you gain.

📘 VOCABULARY

Cost-Benefit Analysis — A decision-making process that compares the total costs of an action (money, time, effort) against the total benefits (value, satisfaction, usefulness).

Opportunity Cost — The value of the next best alternative you give up when making a choice. If you spend $60 on shoes, the opportunity cost might be the $60 you could have saved.

Here's a simple framework you can use for any purchase:

1
List the costs — Purchase price, maintenance, time spent, and what else you could do with that money.
2
List the benefits — Enjoyment, usefulness, how long it'll last, and whether it solves a real problem.
3
Compare — Do the benefits outweigh the costs? Is there a cheaper way to get the same benefit?
4
Decide — Make your choice based on the full picture, not just impulse or emotion.
🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

Should Mia upgrade her phone?

Mia's phone works fine, but a new model just dropped for $899. She earns $480/month from her after-school job in Fort Lauderdale.

COSTS
$899 price (nearly 2 months' income)
Old phone still works fine
Could save that money instead

BENEFITS

When Mia lists it out, the costs are significant and the benefits are mostly cosmetic since her current phone works. The smart move might be to wait until her phone actually needs replacing — and save toward it in the meantime.

SECTION 2
Comparison Shopping and Unit Pricing

Two products might look similar on the shelf, but one could be a much better deal. Smart consumers compare before they buy — not just the sticker price, but the unit price.

📘 VOCABULARY

Comparison Shopping — The practice of comparing prices, features, and quality across different brands or retailers before making a purchase.

Unit Price — The cost per standard unit of measurement (per ounce, per count, per pound). Calculated by dividing the total price by the quantity.

Here's why unit price matters: a 12-pack of water bottles might cost $3.99, while a 24-pack costs $5.99. The 24-pack looks more expensive, but check the unit price:

Total Price
Unit Price
12-pack
$3.99
$0.33/bottle
24-pack
$5.99
$0.25/bottle

The 24-pack is 8 cents cheaper per bottle. Over time, those small savings add up. This same math applies to everything from laundry detergent to streaming subscriptions (monthly vs. annual plans).

Comparison Shopping Tips

Comparison shopping goes beyond just price. Here are key factors to consider:

Price — Compare across multiple stores and online retailers. Don't forget to factor in shipping costs and sales tax.
Quality — A cheaper product that breaks in a month costs more than a pricier one that lasts years. Read reviews from real customers.
Warranty & Return Policy — Can you return it if it doesn't work? Does it come with a warranty? These protections add value.
Total Cost of Ownership — A printer might be cheap, but ink cartridges are expensive. Consider ongoing costs, not just the purchase price.
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

Florida law requires most grocery stores to display unit prices on shelf tags. Next time you're at Publix or Walmart, look at the small print on the shelf label — the unit price is usually listed in the bottom left corner. This makes comparison shopping easy.

SECTION 3
Consumer Rights and Responsibilities

As a consumer, you have legal protections — but you also have responsibilities. Understanding both sides helps you shop with confidence and stand up for yourself when something goes wrong.

Your Consumer Rights

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and state agencies enforce consumer protections. Here are the key rights every consumer has:

Right to Safety — Products must be safe when used as intended. If a product is dangerous, companies must issue recalls.
Right to Be Informed — Companies must provide truthful information about their products. False advertising is illegal.
Right to Choose — You have access to a variety of products and services at competitive prices.
Right to Be Heard — You can file complaints with companies, the Better Business Bureau, or government agencies like the FTC.
Right to Redress — If a product is faulty or a company acts unfairly, you have the right to a fair resolution (refund, replacement, or compensation).
📘 VOCABULARY

Consumer Rights — Legal protections that guarantee fair treatment, truthful information, product safety, and the ability to seek resolution for problems.

Redress — The right to seek a fair remedy (refund, replacement, or compensation) when a product or service fails to meet expectations or legal standards.

Your Consumer Responsibilities

Rights come with responsibilities. Being a smart consumer means doing your part, too:

Research before buying — Read reviews, compare prices, and understand what you're purchasing.
Read the fine print — Understand return policies, warranties, subscription terms, and cancellation fees before you commit.
Report problems — If a product is unsafe or a company is dishonest, report it. Your complaint helps protect other consumers.
Use products as intended — Misusing a product may void your warranty and your legal protections.

SECTION 4
Avoiding Scams and Predatory Practices

Scammers are getting more sophisticated, and young people are increasingly targeted through social media, text messages, and online marketplaces. Knowing the red flags can save you hundreds — or even thousands — of dollars.

Common Scams Targeting Teens and Young Adults
🚩 Fake Job Offers

"Get paid $500/week to be a mystery shopper!" These scams ask you to deposit a check and wire money back. The check bounces, and you're out the money you sent.

🚩 Phishing Messages

Texts or emails pretending to be from your bank, Amazon, or a delivery service. They want you to click a link and enter personal information. Legitimate companies never ask for passwords via text.

🚩 Social Media Shopping Scams

Ads on Instagram or TikTok selling name-brand items at impossibly low prices. You pay, and the product never arrives — or it's a cheap knockoff.

🚩 Free Trial Traps

"Free for 7 days!" but they require your credit card and auto-charge you $49.99/month if you don't cancel in time. The cancellation process is often intentionally difficult.

📘 VOCABULARY

Phishing — A fraudulent attempt to steal personal information by pretending to be a trustworthy source (bank, employer, government agency).

Predatory Practice — A business practice that exploits consumers through deception, hidden fees, or high-pressure tactics designed to benefit the seller at the consumer's expense.

How to Protect Yourself
✓ If a deal seems too good to be true, it probably is
✓ Never share passwords, Social Security numbers, or bank info via text or email
✓ Check website URLs carefully — scam sites often have slight misspellings
✓ Read cancellation terms before signing up for any free trial
✓ Use credit cards (not debit) for online purchases — they offer stronger fraud protection
✓ Report scams to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov or Florida's Attorney General
🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

The Instagram "Brand Deal"

Kenji, a high school junior in Gainesville, received a DM from an account claiming to be a clothing brand. They offered to send him free clothes if he paid $25 for "shipping." Kenji paid — and never received anything. The account was deleted the next day. The red flags? The account had few followers, no verified badge, and asked for payment via Venmo instead of a secure checkout. A legitimate brand deal never asks the influencer to pay.
