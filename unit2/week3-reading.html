How Income Is Earned

Getting a paycheck is exciting — until you see how much smaller it is than you expected. Let's decode where your money goes before it reaches you.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

1 Compare salaries, wages, and other forms of income
2 Explain key payroll deductions and how income tax works
3 Calculate net pay from gross pay using real deduction rates
4 Evaluate how employee benefits add hidden value to compensation

SECTION 1

Salary vs. Wages vs. Everything Else

Not all income is created equal. How you get paid — and what type of income you earn — affects your take-home pay, your schedule, your job protections, and even your tax situation. Let's break down the main types.

💼 SALARY

Fixed annual amount divided into equal paychecks (usually biweekly or monthly).

Same paycheck every period regardless of hours Often "exempt" — no overtime pay Common in professional/office jobs Predictable income for budgeting

Example: $52,000/year ÷ 26 pay periods = $2,000 per paycheck (before deductions)

⏰ WAGES

Hourly rate multiplied by the number of hours worked each pay period.

Pay varies based on hours worked Usually eligible for overtime (1.5x after 40 hrs/week) Common in retail, food service, trades Income can fluctuate week to week

Example: $15/hour × 30 hours = $450 per week (before deductions)

But salary and wages aren't the only ways to earn. Here are other common types of income:

💰

Commission
Earn a percentage of each sale you make (common in sales jobs)

💡

Tips/Gratuities
Extra payment from customers for service (restaurants, salons)

🏗️

Self-Employment
You work for yourself — freelancing, gig work, or running a business

📈

Passive Income
Earnings from investments, rental property, or royalties

VOCABULARY

Salary — A fixed amount of money paid to an employee per year, divided into regular pay periods.

Wages — Payment calculated by multiplying an hourly rate by the number of hours worked.

Commission — Income earned as a percentage of sales made.

Overtime — Pay at 1.5× the regular hourly rate for hours worked beyond 40 per week (for eligible workers).

THINK ABOUT IT

A salaried employee earning $52,000/year works 50 hours a week. A wage worker earning $20/hour works 40 hours and gets overtime after that. If both work 50 hours in a week, who earns more for that week? (Hint: calculate the salaried worker's effective hourly rate.)

CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING

If you could choose between a $45,000 salary with no overtime or a $18/hour wage with overtime eligibility, which factors would you consider before deciding? Think beyond just the dollar amounts.

SECTION 2

Where Does Your Money Go? Payroll Deductions & Taxes

Here's the moment that shocks every first-time worker: your paycheck is significantly smaller than what you earned. If you work 40 hours at $15/hour, you earned $600 — but your check might say $480. Where did the other $120 go?

The answer is payroll deductions — money taken out of your paycheck before you receive it. Some are required by law, others are optional. Understanding the difference between gross pay (what you earned) and net pay (what you actually take home) is one of the most important financial literacy skills you'll learn.

GROSS PAY
$600

What you earned

→
NET PAY
$480

What you take home

The difference: $120 in deductions (taxes, Social Security, Medicare)

Here's what's being deducted and why:

MANDATORY DEDUCTIONS (Required by Law)

Federal Income Tax 10–37%

A percentage of your income paid to the U.S. government. Uses a progressive tax system — the more you earn, the higher your rate on the dollars above each threshold. For most teens with part-time jobs, the rate is 10–12%.

Social Security (FICA) 6.2%

Funds retirement benefits for current and future retirees. Your employer also pays 6.2%, so the total is 12.4% split between you.

Medicare 1.45%

Funds healthcare for people 65 and older. Your employer matches your 1.45% contribution.

State Income Tax Varies

Depends on the state you live in. Some states (like Florida and Texas) have no state income tax. Others charge up to 13%+.

DID YOU KNOW?

Florida is one of nine states with no state income tax. That means if you work in Florida, your mandatory deductions are federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare — that's it. Someone earning the same salary in California would pay an additional 1–13.3% in state taxes.

READING A PAY STUB — EXAMPLE

Here's what a pay stub looks like for a teen working 25 hours/week at $14/hour in Florida (biweekly pay):

Gross Pay (25 hrs × $14 × 2 weeks) $700.00 Federal Income Tax (≈10%) −$70.00 Social Security (6.2%) −$43.40 Medicare (1.45%) −$10.15 State Income Tax (FL = $0) $0.00 Net Pay (Take-Home) $576.45

You earned $700 but took home $576.45 — about 82% of your gross pay. The other 18% went to taxes.

THINK ABOUT IT

Progressive taxes don't mean all your income is taxed at the highest rate. If you earn $50,000, only the dollars above $44,725 are taxed at 22%. The first $11,600 is taxed at 10%, and $11,601–$47,150 is taxed at 12%. Your effective rate (total tax ÷ total income) will be much lower than your top bracket. This is one of the most misunderstood concepts in personal finance.

VOCABULARY

Gross Pay — Total earnings before any deductions are taken out.

Net Pay — Take-home pay after all deductions (taxes, benefits, etc.) have been subtracted.

Payroll Deduction — Any amount subtracted from a paycheck before the employee receives it.

Progressive Tax — A tax system where the rate increases as income rises, applied in brackets.

FICA — Federal Insurance Contributions Act — the law requiring Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) deductions.

CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING

If you earn $800 in gross pay in Florida, estimate your net pay. Use these rates: Federal income tax ≈ 10%, Social Security = 6.2%, Medicare = 1.45%, State tax = 0%. Show your work.

SECTION 3

Beyond the Paycheck: Benefits & Perks

Here's something most people don't realize until they're job hunting: your salary or wage is only part of your total compensation. Benefits — the extras that come with a job — can add 20–40% to the value of your compensation package. A $50,000 salary with great benefits can be worth more than a $60,000 salary with none.

Benefits fall into a few major categories:

🏥

Health Insurance

Employer pays 50–80% of your health, dental, and vision coverage. Without this, individual health insurance can cost $300–$600+/month.

Hidden value: $4,000–$8,000/year

🏖️

Paid Time Off (PTO)

Vacation days, sick days, and holidays where you still get paid. The average American gets about 10 vacation days and 7 paid holidays per year.

Hidden value: $2,000–$5,000/year

📈

Retirement Plans (401k)

Employer matches a percentage of the money you save for retirement. A common match is 50% of your contributions up to 6% of your salary — that's free money.

Hidden value: $1,500–$5,000+/year

🎓

Education Benefits

Tuition reimbursement, student loan assistance, or professional development funding. Some employers pay for your degree entirely.

Hidden value: $2,000–$10,000+/year

REAL-WORLD COMPARISON

Job A: $55,000 salary

+ Health insurance: $6,000

+ 401k match: $2,750

+ PTO (15 days): $3,173

+ Tuition help: $3,000

Total comp: ≈$69,923

Job B: $62,000 salary

+ No health insurance

+ No retirement match

+ PTO (5 days): $1,192

+ No education benefits

Total comp: ≈$63,192

Job A pays $7,000 less in salary but is worth $6,731 MORE in total compensation.

VOCABULARY

Total Compensation — The full value of everything you receive from an employer: salary/wages + benefits + perks.

401(k) — An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan where employees can invest pre-tax income.

Employer Match — When your employer contributes money to your retirement account based on how much you contribute — essentially free money for your future.

DID YOU KNOW?

About 1 in 3 workers don't contribute enough to their 401(k) to get their full employer match — they're literally leaving free money on the table. If your employer matches 5% and you only contribute 2%, you're missing out on 3% of your salary every year.

CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING

Why might a lower-paying job with strong benefits be a smarter financial choice than a higher-paying job without benefits? Give a specific example using health insurance or retirement matching.
