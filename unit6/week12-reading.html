SECTION 1
Why Retirement Planning Starts Now

Retirement might feel like a lifetime away, but here's the math that changes everything: the earlier you start investing for retirement, the less money you need to contribute to end up with more. This is compound growth in action — and it's the single biggest advantage you have right now.

🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

The $600,000 Difference

Both invest $200/month at 8% average return. Who ends up with more?

ELENA: Starts at 22
Invests for 43 years
Total contributed: $103,200
Value at 65: ~$870,000
DAVID: Starts at 32
Invests for 33 years
Total contributed: $79,200
Value at 65: ~$382,000

Elena contributed only $24,000 more than David — but she ended up with nearly $488,000 more. Those first 10 years of compound growth did the heavy lifting. Starting early is the most powerful retirement strategy there is.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

If you started investing just $100/month at age 18 with an 8% return, you'd have over $1,000,000 by age 65. Wait until 28? You'd have about $450,000. That 10-year head start is worth more than $550,000.

SECTION 2
Retirement Account Types

Retirement accounts aren't just regular investment accounts — they come with special tax advantages that help your money grow even faster. The government created these accounts to encourage people to save for retirement.

401(k)
📘 VOCABULARY

401(k) — An employer-sponsored retirement savings plan. Contributions are deducted from your paycheck before taxes, reducing your taxable income. You pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement.

Offered by:Your employer Tax benefit:Pre-tax contributions lower your taxable income now; taxed on withdrawal Key feature:Employer match — FREE money (more on this below) 2025 limit:$23,500/year (under 50) Penalty:10% penalty + taxes if withdrawn before age 59½
Traditional IRA
📘 VOCABULARY

Traditional IRA (Individual Retirement Account) — A personal retirement account you open on your own. Contributions may be tax-deductible (reducing taxable income), and investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal.

Opened by:You (at a brokerage like Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard) Tax benefit:Contributions may be tax-deductible; taxed on withdrawal 2025 limit:$7,000/year (under 50) Penalty:10% penalty + taxes if withdrawn before age 59½
⭐ Roth IRA — The Best Friend of Young Investors
📘 VOCABULARY

Roth IRA — A personal retirement account funded with after-tax money. You don't get a tax break now, but your money grows tax-free and withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.

Opened by:You (at a brokerage); must have earned income Tax benefit:Investments grow tax-free; withdrawals in retirement are TAX-FREE 2025 limit:$7,000/year (under 50) Key advantage:Can withdraw contributions (not gains) anytime without penalty Why it's great for teens:If you're working part-time, you're in a low tax bracket now — pay low taxes today, never pay taxes on the growth!

The Roth IRA is widely considered the best retirement account for young people. Here's why: when you're 17 and earning $8,000/year at a part-time job, you're in the lowest tax bracket. You pay very little tax on your contributions now. Then, your investments grow for 40+ years and you never pay taxes on the growth. That could mean hundreds of thousands in tax-free money.

SECTION 3
Employer-Sponsored Plans and Free Money

When you get a full-time job, one of the most valuable benefits your employer may offer is a 401(k) match. This is literally free money, and not taking advantage of it is one of the biggest financial mistakes young workers make.

📘 VOCABULARY

Employer Match — When an employer contributes money to your 401(k) based on how much you contribute. Example: a "100% match up to 4%" means if you contribute 4% of your salary, your employer adds another 4% — doubling your contribution for free.

🌎 REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

The Power of Employer Match

Jasmine earns $40,000/year. Her employer offers a 100% match up to 5%.

Jasmine contributes 5%:
$2,000/year
Employer matches 100%:
+$2,000/year (FREE)
Total invested per year:
$4,000/year

If Jasmine does NOT contribute to her 401(k), she gets $0 in employer match. She's essentially turning down a $2,000/year raise. Over a 40-year career at 8% return, that employer match alone could grow to over $560,000.

The golden rule of employer matching: always contribute at least enough to get the full employer match. It's a 100% instant return on your money — no investment in the world can guarantee that.

📘 VOCABULARY

Vesting — The process of earning full ownership of employer-contributed funds over time. Some employers require you to stay for 3–5 years before their matching contributions fully belong to you.

🧠 THINK ABOUT IT

Your first job offers a 401(k) with a 50% match up to 6%. You earn $35,000/year. If you contribute 6%, how much does your employer add? What if you only contribute 3%? How much "free money" are you leaving on the table?

SECTION 4
Your Retirement Strategy: A Step-by-Step Plan

Even though retirement is decades away, here's a smart sequence for building long-term wealth:

1
Build an emergency fund first — 3–6 months of expenses in a high-yield savings account. This prevents you from raiding retirement accounts for emergencies.
2
Get the full employer 401(k) match — Contribute at least enough to get every dollar of free money your employer offers.
3
Max out a Roth IRA — Contribute up to $7,000/year for tax-free growth. This is especially powerful while you're young and in a low tax bracket.
4
Increase 401(k) contributions — If you have more to invest, go back to your 401(k) and increase contributions beyond the match.
💡 DID YOU KNOW?

You can open a Roth IRA at any age — as long as you have earned income. If you have a part-time job in high school, you can start a Roth IRA right now. Even $50/month starting at 16 can grow to over $200,000 by retirement.

Vocabulary Review
401(k)
Employer-sponsored retirement plan with pre-tax contributions and potential employer match.
Traditional IRA
Personal retirement account; contributions may be tax-deductible, taxed on withdrawal.
Roth IRA
Personal retirement account; after-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals.
Employer Match
Free money your employer adds to your 401(k) based on your contributions.
Vesting
The process of earning full ownership of employer-contributed retirement funds over time.
Tax-Deferred
Investment growth not taxed until withdrawal (Traditional IRA, 401k).
