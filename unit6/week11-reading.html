SECTION 1
Saving vs. Investing

In Unit 4, you learned how saving puts your money in safe, accessible accounts that earn modest interest. Investing takes a different approach: you put money into assets that have the potential to grow significantly over time — but with more risk.

📘 VOCABULARY

Investing — Putting money into assets (stocks, bonds, real estate, etc.) with the expectation of earning a return over time. Unlike saving, investing involves risk — your money can grow or shrink.

Return — The profit or loss you earn on an investment, usually expressed as a percentage. Also called return on investment (ROI).

The key difference: savings accounts protect your money but barely beat inflation. Investments have the potential to significantly outpace inflation and build real wealth over decades — but they come with the possibility of losing money in the short term.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

The S&P 500 — a collection of 500 large U.S. companies — has averaged about a 10% annual return over the last 90+ years. That means $1,000 invested would grow to roughly $6,727 in 20 years. In a savings account at 4%, that same $1,000 would only reach $2,191.

SECTION 2
Types of Investments
Stocks
📘 VOCABULARY

Stock (Equity) — A share of ownership in a company. When you buy stock, you own a tiny piece of that business and share in its profits (or losses).

Dividend — A portion of a company's profits paid out to shareholders, usually quarterly. Not all stocks pay dividends.

When you buy a share of stock, you become a part-owner of that company. You make money two ways: the stock price goes up (capital gains), or the company pays you dividends. Stocks offer the highest potential return of common investment types — but also the highest risk.

Potential returns: 8%–12% average annually (long-term)
Risk level: HIGH — individual stocks can lose 50%+ or even become worthless
Bonds
📘 VOCABULARY

Bond — A loan you make to a government or corporation. They pay you back with interest over a set period. Bonds are essentially IOUs — you're the lender.

When you buy a bond, you're lending money to the issuer (a company or government). In return, they pay you regular interest payments and return your principal at the end of the bond's term. Bonds are generally safer than stocks but offer lower returns.

Potential returns: 3%–6% average annually
Risk level: LOW to MODERATE — government bonds are very safe; corporate bonds carry more risk
Mutual Funds
📘 VOCABULARY

Mutual Fund — A professionally managed pool of money from many investors, used to buy a diversified mix of stocks, bonds, or other assets. Managed by a fund manager who makes buying/selling decisions.

Mutual funds let you invest in hundreds of companies at once without having to pick individual stocks. A professional fund manager decides which investments to buy and sell. The downside? Management fees (called expense ratios) can eat into your returns.

Potential returns: varies by type (stock fund ~8%–10%, bond fund ~3%–5%)
Risk level: MODERATE — diversification reduces risk, but fees may reduce returns
ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)
📘 VOCABULARY

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund) — Similar to a mutual fund but traded on a stock exchange like an individual stock. ETFs typically track an index (like the S&P 500) and have lower fees than mutual funds.

Index Fund — A mutual fund or ETF that tracks a specific market index (like the S&P 500) rather than being actively managed. Known for low fees and consistent long-term performance.

ETFs are one of the most popular investment choices for beginners and experts alike. A single share of an S&P 500 ETF gives you exposure to 500 large companies at once. They trade like stocks (you can buy and sell throughout the day) but offer the diversification of a mutual fund — usually with much lower fees.

Potential returns: matches the index it tracks (~10% avg. for S&P 500 ETFs)
Risk level: MODERATE — diversified but still subject to market fluctuations

SECTION 3
Risk vs. Return

Every investment involves a trade-off between risk and return. This is one of the most fundamental principles in finance: the higher the potential return, the higher the risk. There's no such thing as a high-return, no-risk investment — and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

📘 VOCABULARY

Risk — The possibility that an investment will lose value or not perform as expected.

Risk Tolerance — Your personal willingness and ability to accept potential losses in exchange for higher potential gains. Factors: age, income, goals, personality.

Your risk tolerance depends on several factors. Young investors (like you) generally have a higher risk tolerance because you have decades for your investments to recover from downturns. Someone retiring in 5 years needs to be more conservative because they can't afford big losses right before they need the money.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?

The stock market has experienced major drops — losing 30–50% in some years. But it has always recovered and continued to grow over time. The S&P 500 has never had a negative return over any 20-year period in its history. This is why time in the market beats trying to time the market.

SECTION 4
Diversification: Don't Put All Your Eggs in One Basket
📘 VOCABULARY

Diversification — Spreading your investments across different asset types, industries, and geographies to reduce risk. If one investment drops, others may hold steady or increase.

Portfolio — The collection of all your investments — stocks, bonds, funds, and other assets — considered together.

Diversification is one of the most important concepts in investing. The idea is simple: if you spread your money across many different investments, a loss in one area can be offset by gains in another.

🌎 REAL-WORLD SCENARIO

Concentrated vs. Diversified

Two students each invest $2,000:

STUDENT A: All-In
Puts all $2,000 in one tech stock. The company loses a lawsuit and the stock drops 60%. Student A loses $1,200.
STUDENT B: Diversified
Puts $2,000 in an S&P 500 index fund (500 companies). That same tech stock drops 60%, but it's a tiny fraction of the fund. The overall fund drops only 2%. Student B loses $40.

Same amount invested. Same bad event. But diversification turned a $1,200 loss into a $40 loss. That's the power of not putting all your eggs in one basket.

This is exactly why index funds and ETFs are so popular with both beginner and expert investors: they provide instant diversification. One purchase gives you exposure to hundreds or thousands of companies.
